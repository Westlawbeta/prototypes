Found 4 resources:

  1. Espinoza v. CGJC Holdings LLC United States District Court, • S.D. New York July 23, 2024 • 23cv9133 (DLC) Even assuming posting to Instagram is a “recreational activity” under the NYLL, “the complaint alleges that the plaintiff was discharged, not for the activity of [posting to Instagram], but for the content of the [ ] post.” Court granted defendants’ motion to dismiss claim for wrongful termination under § 201-d(2)(c).
  2. Davi v. Roberts E.D.N.Y • 2021 • 523 F.Supp. 3d 295 The court ruled that Davi's social media comments were protected under the First Amendment, finding no sufficient evidence that his comments disrupted agency operations, and concluded the discipline was retaliatory.
  3. MasTec Advanced Technologies, a Division of MasTec, Inc. N.L.R.B-BD • 2011 • 357 N.L.R.B Employee's communications with the public may lose the protection of the Act if they are sufficiently disloyal or defamatory.
  4. Three D, LLC v. Nat’l Labor Relations Bd. United States District Court, 2d Cir. • 2015 • 629 F. App’x 33 Employee's social media comments, which included support for a union and criticisms of management, were protected under the NLRA.
